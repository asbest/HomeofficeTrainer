Idea in 30 seconds

A regular bicycle + roller trainer + generator = power for your desk. Many people can pedal 60–100 W for 30–60 minutes. With a small buffer battery and basic electronics you can run a laptop (~50 W) and a monitor (~25 W)—at least intermittently or as a mix of battery + pedaling.

Note: Net output depends on rider, ergonomics and electronics. Budget for a 50–70 % overall efficiency from pedal to usable electricity.

Variant A – Budget & simple

Core: 24 V DC permanent‑magnet motor as generator (e.g., scooter/treadmill), 12 V LiFePO4 battery as buffer, USB‑C PD car charger for laptop. Optional small pure‑sine inverter (150–300 W) for 230 V.

Variant B – Comfort & feature‑rich

Core: E‑bike hub motor as 3‑phase generator + rectifier + MPPT charge controller (12/24 V) for optimum yield. LiFePO4 battery + pure sine inverter 300–600 W for mains devices.

Bigger buffer MPPT for efficiency

Variant C – Grid feed‑in (with professional)

Only with a certified micro‑inverter (e.g., balcony PV class) and a licensed electrician: Generator → DC conditioning into PV window → micro inverter → wall socket. Anti‑islanding & standards required.

Warning: Grid‑parallel operation must comply with standards (e.g., VDE‑AR‑N 4105 in DE) and utility rules. Involve a professional.

Starter BOM: Bracket + generator only (low cost)

  • Roller trainer (wheel‑on, used): e.g., Tacx Blue Matic T2650 – stable bike mount.
  • Generator: MY1016 brushed DC motor, 24 V/300 W (scooter motor) – direct DC output.
  • Timing belt drive: HTD‑5M, 15 mm + 2 pulleys (near 1:1 ratio; ~20T / 50–60T per roller rpm).
  • Motor bracket: 3–4 mm aluminum angle with slots for belt tension, M6/M8 hardware.
  • Guard: belt cover (sheet/wood/3D‑print) – keep fingers & clothes safe.
  • Test load (optional): 12 V/21 W auto bulb + multimeter for first checks.
Tip: For the first spin you only need the mechanics. Add DC regulator/battery/USB‑C later.

Build steps (plain language)

Stabilize the bike

Mount the bicycle on a solid wheel‑on trainer. Normal tire pressure, clean rear tire. Ensure zero wobble.

Stable stance Check axle clamps

Attach the generator

Install the friction roller (or belt drive) so it presses firmly—but not excessively—on the tire. A simple L‑bracket made from aluminum/wood is often enough.

Correct contact pressure Check alignment

Rectify & charge

Variant A: DC motor already outputs DC—use a buck/boost to regulate to 13.8–14.4 V for LiFePO4.
Variant B: Rectify the hub motor’s 3‑phase AC with a bridge rectifier, then feed an MPPT charge controller.

Fuse on + line Observe polarity

Buffer battery & outputs

Connect the battery through a fuse (e.g., 10 A on a 12 V system). Add a USB‑C PD 100 W output for laptops. Optionally add a pure sine inverter for 230 V devices.

USB‑C PD 230 V (optional)

First power‑up

Measure open‑circuit voltage, then add a small load (e.g., 20 W). Pedal smoothly at 60–90 rpm; watch voltage/temperature.

Target: ~50–70 W output Quiet operation

Fine‑tune load

Use gear choice and the DC regulator to keep power constant (e.g., ~70 W). Avoid overly heavy load—inefficient and tiring.

Optional: Grid feed‑in (Variant C)

Only with a certified micro‑inverter and a professional. Match generator DC to the inverter’s PV input; connect per code to socket/sub‑panel.

Safety: 12–24 V DC can still be hazardous (shorts/heat). 230 V AC requires proper protection. Use adequate wire gauge (≥2.5 mm² above 10 A), automotive fuses and strain relief.

Performance check & sizing

  • Human output: many can sustain 60–100 W for 30–60 min; trained riders more. Be conservative.
  • Cable & fuse: 100 W at 12 V → ~8.5 A. Use 2.5 mm² wire, 10 A fuse.
  • Battery size: 12 V × 20 Ah ≈ 240 Wh (usable ~200 Wh). Enough for ~2–3 h laptop time (frugal).
  • Efficiency chain (example): Generator 75 % × rectifier/regulator 90 % × battery 95 % × inverter 88 % ≈ ~56 %.

Tips & troubleshooting

  • Belt/contact pressure: Avoid slip with a dry tire and sufficient pressure without tire squirm.
  • Hot parts: Put rectifier/regulator on a heatsink; add a fan if needed.
  • Inverter buzz: Smooth the load (battery between generator and inverter).
  • Laptop drops out: Set USB‑C PD to 20 V/5 A (100 W); use a quality cable.
  • Noise: Rubberized/damped roller, grease bearings.

Grid feed‑in & legal

If you want to feed into the household grid: use only a certified micro‑inverter (anti‑islanding, grid‑code compliant) and a licensed electrician. Follow your country’s standards (e.g., VDE‑AR‑N 4105 in Germany) and utility requirements. Improper feed‑in is dangerous and prohibited.
